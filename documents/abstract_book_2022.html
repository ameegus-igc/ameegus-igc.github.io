Abeer Heskol

Keywords: Neurogenesis, Transcription regulation, multi-omics

Characterization of the Notch cistrome in vertebrate neurogenesis

Abeer Heskol (1, 2), Lígia Tavares (1, 2) and Diogo S. Castro (1,2)

(1) Instituto Gulbenkian de Ciência, Oeiras, Portugal

(2) i3S – Institute of Research and Innovation in Health, University of Porto, Portugal

The Notch pathway is important during the development of many tissues, where it controls the balance between stem cell maintenance and differentiation. In the embryonic vertebrate nervous system, the Notch pathway plays a major role regulating the rate of neuronal differentiation of neural stem/progenitor cells during the neurogenic phase. In spite of its well characterized function, little is still known on the regulatory rules underlying the Notch transcriptional response. Here, I will present our ongoing work aiming at characterizing the cis-regulation of this important pathway during neurogenesis, by applying a genomics approach to identify Notch-responsive regulatory regions, and associated target genes. Studies are focused on the ventral telencephalon, a well-established model region of embryonic neurogenesis. We are combining the genome-wide mapping of core components of the pathway and relevant histone marks, with the interactome of active regulatory regions and transcriptional profiling. This is applied to embryonic brain material, or to neural stem cells cultures, where Notch signalling can be manipulated. Integration of various data sets will address how the cis-regulatory architecture contributes to determining the transcriptional response to the Notch pathway.

 

Ana Milas

Keywords: Development; Cell polarity; Par proteins

Oocyte polarity in Drosophila is actively maintained by posterior follicle cells

Ana Milas, Jorge de-Carvalho and Ivo Telley

Instituto Gulbenkian de Ciência, Oeiras, Portugal

The formation of the anterior–posterior body axis in Drosophila requires signalling from posterior follicle cells (PFCs) to the stage-7 oocyte, leading to asymmetric localization of the partitioning defective (Par) proteins. This causes polarization of the microtubule network, which directs kinesin-dependent transport of oskar mRNA to the posterior. Although several studies have concluded the necessity of signalling using genetic approaches, neither the nature of the signal nor the possible role of the PFCs in the following stages of oogenesis are known.

To directly test the role of PFCs in the maintenance of polarization, we laser ablated PFCs. This resulted in localization of previously excluded anterior polarity protein, Par3/Bazooka, to the posterior membrane. Interestingly, posterior Par protein, Par1, was not excluded from this membrane prior to the accumulation of Bazooka, suggesting that antagonism between Bazooka and Par1 is not sufficient for their mutual exclusion. Par-1 did get excluded from the posterior over longer timescales, which was followed by local accumulation of microtubules.

Furthermore, we observed that during stages of oogenesis in which Bazooka is excluded from the posterior membrane, the oocyte is tightly connected to the PFCs, i.e. a clear boundary between the oocyte and the PFCs is not detectable in light microscopy images. We suspect that the boundaries visibly disappear because increased trafficking between those cells occurs, which has a ruffling effect on the cortical membrane.

To test whether the contact between PFCs and the oocyte is necessary to maintain exclusion of Bazooka, we developed an assay to mechanically detach PFCs from the oocyte. This detachment led to the recruitment of Bazooka to the posterior end. Incidentally, posterior localization of Bazooka coincided with the appearance of a visible extracellular gap at the oocyte–PFC interface, suggesting that cell-cell adhesion between germline and somatic cells is necessary to maintain polarity.

In conclusion, we show for the first time that PFCs are important to maintain polarization of Par network and the microtubule cytoskeleton in the oocyte. This enables delivery of oskar mRNA to the posterior of the oocyte, a process which critically depends on polarization of the microtubule network. We propose that polarity maintenance is achieved through a cell adhesion dependent mechanism.

 

Ana Teresa Mendes Eugénio

Keywords: transposable elements dynamics, genetic variation, evolution

Wolbachia effects on Transposable Element mobilization

Eugénio, AT (1); Beldade, P (2)

(1) Instituto Gulbenkian De Ciência, Oeiras, Portugal;

(2) CE3C - Centre For Ecology, Evolution And Environmental Changes, FCUL University Of Lisbon, Lisbon, Portugal

Wolbachia is a maternally transmitted bacterial endosymbiont that is widespread across insects, arachnids and nematodes. This endosymbiont affects many aspects of host biology and can play a role in host defense. Wolbachia confers resistance against viral infection and affects abundance of piRNAs, which is an important mechanism to repress transposable element (TE) activity in the germline of eukaryotes. To test the hypothesis that Wolbachia impacts TE mobilization in host genomes, we studied TE expression in female flies from a panel of Drosophila melanogaster lines naturally infected with Wolbachia and for which we have information on TE insertions. We removed Wolbachia from 25 D. melanogaster genotypes from the Drosophila Genetic Reference Panel, to create a panel of 25 pairs of Wolbachia-positive and Wolbachia-negative lines. Then, we characterized Wolbachia loads in adults from the Wolbachia-positive genotypes and measured the transcription of 14 TEs of different families in adults from each of the Wolbachia-positive and Wolbachia negative genotypes. We found variation in TE expression, depending on Wolbachia status, type of TE, and host genotype. Differences in TE expression with and without Wolbachia mostly depended on host genotype, rather than on TE identity. These data suggest that Wolbachia may impact TE mobilization and the generation of novel genetic variants in host genomes.

Role of Olfactory Receptors in Infection - The case of Olfr56

André Boler Barros1,2, Kátia Jesus1, Marc Veldhoen3, Luís Ferreira Moita1

(1) Instituto Gulbenkian de Ciência, 2780-156 Oeiras, Portugal

(2) Instituto de Tecnologia Química e Biológica, Universidade Nova de Lisboa, 2780-157

Oeiras, Portugal

(3) Instituto de Medicina Molecular João Lobo Antunes, 1649-028 Lisboa, Portugal

Olfactory receptors are a key component on detecting and reacting to the environment. Apart from this role, these G-Protein Coupled Receptors are present in non-sensory organism and cells, as well as involved in non-sensory pathways. Specifically in the context of infection, these receptors have been showed to be expressed in immune cells. They are also involved in sensing pathogen-metabolites, such as acetate.

Transcriptomic data has shown some Olfactory Receptors are highly regulated upon infection. The one with the most substantial change is Olfr56. This receptor has a strong upregulation, both on mRNA and on protein levels, during the 24h period post- infection. In addition, this upregulation seems to be characteristic to several immune cell types.

Using a transgenic mouse with a non-functional version of the protein, we have tested several in vivo infection models. On this context, only the Cecum Ligation and Puncture (CLP) model has shown interesting results, with increased survival for the Olfr56-/-. Within vitro stimulation with (LPS), we have observed a gender-variable regulation of proinflammatory cytokines, such as IL1b.

The results of this project can further improve our understanding of the role of olfactory receptors in immune response. Plus, considering the pharmacological importance of GPCRs, this project may open new avenues on infection treatment.

 

Carla Filipa Simões Henriques

Keywords: Neuroscience; Behaviour; Genetics

Functional specialization of social and asocial learnings in Drosophila melanogaster

Carla Simões-Henriques1; Joana Marcos1; Élio Sucena1; Maria L. Vasconcelos2; Susana A. M. Varela1,3*; Rui F. Oliveira1,2,3*

(1) Instituto Gulbenkian de Ciência, Oeiras, Portugal;

(2) Champalimaud Centre for the Unknown, Lisboa, Portugal;

(3) ISPA-Istituto Universitário, Lisboa, Portugal;

*PI and co-PI of the SocialFly Project, respectively

Social and asocial learnings are required to cope with the complexity of the environment. They are behaviourally distinct. However, whether they use a single (general-purpose) or distinct (special-purpose) cognitive mechanisms remains elusive. A general-purpose mechanism comprises a single set of genes regulating the same biochemical pathways and neural circuits, while a special-purpose mechanism comprises two sets of genes, possibly affecting different biochemical pathways and/or neural circuits.

To address this question, we firstly tested social and asocial learning abilities in 40 lines of the DGRP, a panel of several isogenic sequenced lines that together represent the genetic variation of a natural Drosophila population. For asocial learning, individual flies had to learn to avoid an oviposition-site where they associated an odour (conditioned stimulus, CS) with a bitter taste (unconditioned stimulus, US). For social learning, flies had to learn the oviposition-site choice of a group of conspecifics previously exposed to the CS and the US. Secondly, we performed a Genome-Wide Association Study – a statistical correlation between the genetic variants in the DGRP and each learning phenotype. We obtained two completely different sets of candidate genes for social and asocial learnings. Thirdly, to functionally validate the role of each gene on each learning phenotype, we used GAL4/RNAi-UAS lines to knockdown the genes. We found genes that affect both learnings and also cases of specialization.

This is the first experimental evidence supporting the special-purpose cognitive mechanism hypothesis. Future work should focus on the extent of this specialization, concerning the biochemical pathways and the neural circuitry.

Financial support was also granted by ASAB, the Association for the Study of Animal Behaviour.

 

Christian Diwo

Keywords: Membrane protein biogenesis, viral infection

How membrane proteins encode their fate to reach the membrane - the determinants of membrane protein translocation route selection at the ER

Christian Diwo, Colin Adrain and Maria-João Amorim

Instituto Gulbenkian De Ciência, Oeiras, Portugal;

Biological membranes are functionalized through the dense population with membrane spanning proteins which is a fundamental requisite for life. In modern eukaryotic cells around 1/4 of all proteins synthesized in the cytosol are inserted into the endoplasmic reticulum (ER) membrane by broadly conserved protein complexes. The Sec61 protein conducting channel is often the center piece in various assemblages that facilitate the membrane insertion or translocation of physico-chemical diverse membrane proteins but recently more ER membrane components have been found to act as alternative translocation routes. Many molecular observations in the chain of events from protein translation, to ER membrane targeting of translating ribosomes, and finally protein translocation kinetics contribute to our current understanding of membrane protein biogenesis, however it remains unclear how a specific protein influences its own translocation route. This is important as membrane protein biogenesis is inherently error prone and may lead to disease when quality control mechanisms are failing. I am developing biochemical and proteomic tools with which I aim to answer how protein import at the ER is concerted by its various translocation routes and impacted by conditions like viral insults. We observe that a small viral membrane protein M2 of the influenza A virus (IAV) is dependent on an alternative membrane insertion pathway, the endoplasmic reticulum membrane protein complex (EMC). Previous reports show that the M2-like membrane protein architecture can be dependent on either Sec61 or EMC processing, which allows us to rationally design mutants that switch their dependencies. In order to gain statistical power in discriminating protein features that determine translocation route I am developing a labelling approach based on the ER luminal expression of a promiscuous biotin ligase. By labelling imported proteins with biotin we will be able to interrogate protein import at the ER on a proteome scale under conditions where a certain translocation route is ablated or monitor protein import changes during IAV or SARS-CoV 2 infection.

Keywords: SARS-CoV-2; Evolution; Mutation rates

Mutation rate of SARS-CoV-2 and emergence of mutators during experimental evolution.

Amicone, M (1); Borges, V (2); Alves, MJ (3); Isidro, J (2); Zé-Zé, L (3,4); Duarte, S (5); Vieira, L (5,6); Guiomar, R (7); Gomes, JP (2); Gordo, I (1).

(1) IGC – Instituto Gulbenkian de Ciência, Oeiras, Portugal;

(2) INSA – Bioinformatics Unit, Department of Infectious Diseases, National Institute of Health Doutor Ricardo Jorge, Lisbon, Portugal;

(3) INSA – Centre for Vectors and Infectious Diseases Research, Department of Infectious Diseases, National Institute of Health Doutor Ricardo Jorge, Águas de Moura, Portugal;

(4) BioISI – Biosystems & Integrative Sciences Institute, Faculty of Sciences, University of Lisbon, Portugal;

(5) INSA – Innovation and Technology Unit, Department of Human Genetics, National Institute of Health Doutor Ricardo Jorge, Lisbon, Portugal;

(6) ToxOmics – Centre for Toxicogenomics and Human Health, Genetics, Oncology and Human Toxicology, Nova Medical School|Faculdade de Ciências Médicas, Universidade Nova de Lisboa, Lisbon, Portugal;

(7) INSA – National Reference Laboratory for Influenza and other Respiratory Viruses, Department of Infectious Diseases, National Institute of Health Doutor Ricardo Jorge, Lisbon, Portugal.

To understand how organisms evolve, it is fundamental to study how mutations emerge and establish. Here, we estimated the rate of mutation accumulation of SARS-CoV-2 in vitro and investigated the repeatability of its evolution when facing a new cell type but no immune or drug pressures. We performed experimental evolution with two strains of SARS-CoV-2, one carrying the originally described spike protein (CoV-2-D) and another carrying the D614G mutation that has spread worldwide (CoV-2-G). After 15 passages in Vero cells and whole genome sequencing, we characterized the spectrum and rate of the emerging mutations and looked for evidence of selection across the genomes of both strains. From the frequencies of the mutations accumulated, and excluding the genes with signals of selection, we estimate a spontaneous mutation rate of 1.3x10-6 ± 0.2x10-6 per-base per-infection cycle (mean across both lineages of SARS-CoV-2 ± 2SEM). We further show that mutation accumulation is larger in the CoV-2-D lineage and heterogeneous along the genome, consistent with the action of positive selection on the spike protein. We also observe the emergence of mutators in the CoV-2-G background, likely linked to mutations in the RNA-dependent RNA polymerase and/or in the error-correcting exonuclease protein. These results provide valuable information on how spontaneous mutations emerge in SARS-CoV-2 and on how selection can shape its genome towards adaptation to new environments.

 

Patricia Mónica Silva Costa

Keywords: Plant biology, root development

SnRK1 dual function in lateral root development

Monica Costa, Tom Beeckman, Elena Baena-Gonzalez

Plants for Life PhD program ITQB Nova / Root Development lab - VIB / Plant Stress Signaling group - IGC

One major strategy of plants to adapt to highly heterogeneous and often challenging environments is to modify root architecture. To this end, dedicated signaling pathways integrate environmental cues with hard-wired developmental programs into a unified response that tailors growth in accordance with the environment. How this integration occurs is poorly understood but has been proposed to involve carbon signaling through the Sucrose non-fermenting Related Kinase 1 (SnRK1). In agreement, SnRK1 knockdown mutants show defective repression of lateral root (LR) formation in response to ABA (a major stress hormone), suggesting that SnRK1 is important for readjusting root architecture in situations of stress. To elucidate the mechanisms underlying SnRK1 effect, we are currently undertaking several complementary strategies. Firstly, we aim to identify the stage of LR development targeted by SnRK1. LRs form by proliferation of specific pericycle cells that differentiate into a new organ. This process can be divided into 4 steps: founder cell specification, initiation, primordium formation, emergence, and meristem activation. ABA has been implicated in several of these developmental steps. Phenotyping of constitutive SnRK1 mutants and an inducible loss-of-function line suggest that, under normal conditions, SnRK1 stimulates early development of LR, prior to initiation, whilst in the presence of ABA, SnRK1 inhibits post emergence LR elongation. We have also uncovered an impact of SnRK1 in primary root growth, in what seems to be a different role to the one observed in LRs. Altogether, our results highlight a major role for SnRK1 in shaping root architecture.

 

Patrícia Rodrigues Duarte

Keywords: Embryonic development

Evaluating the switch in cellular competence during head to trunk transition

Patrícia Duarte, André Dias, Ana Nóvoa, Moisés Mallo

Instituto Gulbenkian de Ciência, Oeiras, Portugal

Development of vertebrate embryos is characterized by early formation of the anterior tissues and sequential axial extension at their posterior end, first by the activity of the primitive streak and then of the tail bud. Head and trunk development are significantly different, and this transition involves a change in the capacity of cells to respond to particular gene networks when entering the trunk formation stage. Genetic experiments indicate that retinoic acid is crucial during this transition phase, becoming dispensable once trunk formation has started. Herein, we aim to understand the mechanisms behind the switch in cell competence during head to trunk transition and the role that retinoic acid plays in this process. For this, we performed a genome wide chromatin accessibility assay to identify regulatory regions involved in this transition, followed by their functional assessment using transgenic and genome editing strategies in mouse embryos. This approach allowed us to identify a novel enhancer regulating the expression of Hoxa5. Moreover, transcription factor footprinting analysis revealed the presence of a VDR (Vitamin D Receptor) binding site involved in the regulation of this enhancer.

 

Renato José Melo Sosua

Keywords: Neurobiology, Dopamine, Learning

Towards an understanding of parallel learning systems during decisions in a dynamic value landscape

Renato Sousa (1,2), Rui F. Oliveira (1, 2, 3), Joseph J. Paton (2)

(1) Instituto Gulbenkian de Ciência

(2) Champalimaud Neuroscience Programme

(3) ISPA - Instituo Universitário, Lisboa, Portugal

Animals strive to maximize returns by either acquiring rewards or avoiding punishments. Accurately predicting what actions best lead to desired outcomes is thus a fundamental skill, well-grounded in the function of midbrain dopamine (DA) neurons. However, DA neurons are embedded in a circuit architecture that constrains the type of information they have access to and, consequently, their function. Along the striatum, projections from DA neurons converge with cortico-striatal synapses to form a tripartite unit where values of actions are materialized. Though, it remains poorly understood whether the role of this tripartite unit for learning depends on striatal anatomy or not.

In this project, we address these parallel learning systems by recording and perturbing individual components of this unit in functionally well-defined striatal domains. We show that mice performing a probabilistic task use their recent trial history to determine what actions are most valuable and adapt their choices. We observe that DA terminals in the dorsolateral striatum (DLS) scale their responses with a relative value gradient for choices that result in a reward. Lastly, we also demonstrate that optogenetic activation of indirect pathway neurons (iMSNs) in the tail of the striatum (TS) increases the probability of choosing the ipsilateral option.

In natural settings, mice face complex decisions that result from the multidimensionality of the environment they inhabit. To better approximate our task to a more ethological circumstance, we will complement it with an odor compound derived from the urine of a naturally occurring predator. We expect this additional stimulus of opposing value will enhance the contrast between potential action outcomes and, therefore, invoke activity in DA systems that best resembles the patterns underlying learning in naturally occurring scenarios.

 

Sónia Maria Leite Pereira

Keywords: Malaria, Immunity, Infection, Disease 

Sónia Pereira et al.,

Instituto de Tecnologia Química e Biológica António Xavier, Universidade Nova de Lisboa (ITQB - NOVA), Oeiras, Portugal 

Malaria is still a major health concern in the XXI century. Being responsible for about half a  million deaths every year, malaria´s incidence, stretching across vast geographic areas, puts  nearly half of the world’s population at risk. In the mammalian host, the life cycle of  Plasmodium encompasses two distinct replicative stages. The first, considered clinically silent  despite obligatory, occurs in the liver, where the parasite invades and replicates within the  hepatocyte, i.e., the liver-stage (LS); and the second, initiated once the liver-born parasitic  progeny reaches the blood and infects the red blood cell (RBC), i.e., the blood-stage (BS),  which is associated with the emergence of clinical manifestations and disease. During malarial  infections, parasite density results from the interplay between immunity against Plasmodium  and virulence mechanisms of the parasite. Overall, the balance between parasite elimination  and persistence has a major impact on the clinical outcome of infection, with parasite burden  being currently the best-known correlate of disease severity following Plasmodium infections  in humans. We took the view that the LS of infection, albeit silent, may influence parasite  virulence and/or host-driven immune mechanisms aiming at parasite elimination, ultimately  precluding effective control of parasite density. By using a rodent model of interrupted  hepatic infection followed by an infectious BS, we have now established that this is the case.  The establishment of Plasmodium parasites in the liver prior to a BS infection is correlated  with decreased removal of parasitized RBCs by the spleen, and effect that is, at least partially,  dependent on T cells and/or B cells. In the future, we intend to unveil the mechanistic basis  of this critical aspect of host-Plasmodium interactions where the non-symptomatically LS of  infection impacts on parasite virulence and, ultimately, on the pathological outcome of  infection.

Keywords: Infectious Diseases, Metabolism

A role of Heme in Immunity against Malaria

Temitope Wilson Ademolue (1), Susana Ramos (1), Elisa Jentho (1,2), Qian Wu (1), Joel Guerra (2), Rui Martins (1), Gil Pires (1), Sebastian Weis (2,3,4), Ana Rita Carlos (1), Inês Mahú (1), Elsa Seixas (1), Denise Duarte (5), Fabienne Rajas (6), Sílvia Cardoso (1), António G. G. Sousa (1), Jingtao Lilue (1), Gilles Mithieux (6), Fátima Nogueira (5) and Miguel P. Soares (1)

(1) Instituto Gulbenkian de Ciência, Oeiras, Portugal.

(2) Department of Anesthesiology and Intensive Care Medicine, Jena University Hospital, Friedrich-Schiller-University, Jena, Germany.

(3) Institute for Infectious Disease and Infection Control, University Hospital Jena, Germany.

(4) Center for Sepsis Control and Care, Jena University, Germany.

(5) Global Health and Tropical Medicine, Instituto de Higiene e Medicina Tropical, Universidade NOVA de Lisboa, Lisboa, Portugal.

(6) Université Claude Bernard Lyon, INSERM, France.

Hypoglycemia is a clinical hallmark of severe malaria, the often-lethal presentation of Plasmodium falciparum infection of humans. Here we report that malaria-associated hypoglycemia emerges from a hypometabolic-based defense strategy whereby the infected host reduces blood glucose levels. This occurs via a coordinated response elicited by labile heme, a byproduct of hemolysis, which induces anorexia and represses hepatic glucose production (HGP). While protective against unfettered immune-mediated inflammation, organ damage and anemia, repression of HGP can progress towards hypoglycemia when sustained over time, compromising host energy expenditure and thermoregulation. This hypometabolic state arrests the development of asexual stages of Plasmodium spp., which undergo pyknosis, associated with mitochondrial dysfunction. In response, Plasmodium activates a transcriptional program reducing virulence and inducing sexual differentiation towards transmissible gametocytes. In conclusion, malaria-associated hypoglycemia represents a trade-off of an evolutionarily conserved defense strategy restricting Plasmodium spp. from accessing host-derived glucose and balancing parasite virulence and transmission.

Camila Veludo Ramos

Keywords: Immunology, Cell biology

IL-7r is not required at the DN3e in steady state thymopoiesis

Camila Ramos and Vera Martins

Instituto Gulbenkian de Ciênciia, Oeiras, Portugal

T lymphocyte differentiation requires interleukin 7 (IL-7)/IL-7 receptor (IL-7r) signaling to ensure the expansion of T cell precursors prior to key selection events. IL-7r deficient mice are lymphopenic, and immature thymocytes lacking IL-7r have severe proliferation and survival defects. This compromises the generation of double-negative 3 early (DN3e) cells, but whether DN3e require IL-7r signaling has yet to be addressed. Here, by conditionally deleting the IL-7r locus at the DN3e, we showed that DN3e can respond to increase in IL-7 availability by proliferating. However, in a competitive setting, IL-7r deficient DN3e achieved a cellularity similar to that of wild-type DN3e, with comparable levels of apoptosis and proliferation. Taken together, our results suggest that DN3e do not respond to IL-7 unless its uptake by other T cell precursors is reduced.

 

Catarina Sofia Rodrigues do Carmo

Keywords: Cell Biology

Lodestar, a protein with a dual role in mitotic transcription inactivation and sister chromatid resolution

Catarina Carmo, João Coelho, Abha Bose and Raquel Oliveira

Instituto Gulbenkian de Ciência, Rua da Quinta Grande, 2780-156 Oeiras, Portugal 

Mitosis is essential for life. This process requires drastic changes in chromosome structure that parallels with a major switch off in transcriptional activity. While it has long been assumed that increased compaction would render mitotic chromatin inaccessible for transcription, the interplay between mitotic chromosome organization and transcription termination remains elusive.

While looking for novel genetic interactions with chromosome assembly factors, we uncovered a poorly characterized helicase-like protein – TTF2 (transcription termination factor 2) / Lds (lodestar in Drosophila melanogaster).

We found that Lds is predominantly cytoplasmic during interphase and localizes to mitotic chromosomes a few seconds prior to Nuclear Envelope Breakdown, suggesting it may be critical for mitosis-specific chromatin events. Accordingly, absence of Lds results in severe segregation errors (1), mostly characterized by chromatin bridges during anaphase. However, how Lds ensures faithful chromosome segregation remains unknown.

Given that absence of Lds results in severe chromatin bridges, we hypothesize that it aids in the process of sister chromatid resolution. Accordingly, we found that Lds interacts with the major enzyme that resolves sister chromatid intertwines - Topoisomerase II – and localizes to chromatin bridges at mitotic exit. These findings suggest Lds cooperates with Topoisomerase II to facilitate sister chromatid resolution.

Strikingly, previous studies support that Lds holds transcription termination activity in vitro (2), which would indicate that the observed segregation defects could be (at least partially) attributed to impairment in transcription termination. Indeed, we show that Lds is required for timely release of transcripts from chromatin at mitotic entry. Using a system to monitor ongoing transcription in real time (3) we see that in embryos with reduced levels of Lds retain transcripts at mitotic chromatin, despite normal mitotic progression and chromosome compaction.

We propose that Lds has a dual role during mitosis, by ensuring prompt inactivation of transcription and cooperating in sister chromatid resolution. Ongoing work is aiming to dissect how these two functions are mechanistically linked and the contribution of these two processes for mitotic fidelity.

 

Hugo Manuel Condessa Barreto

Keywords: Evolution, Microbiology, Antibiotics

Gut mélange à trois: fluctuating selection modulated by microbiota, host immune system, and antibiotics

Hugo C. Barreto, Beatriz Abreu and Isabel Gordo

Instituto Gulbenkian de Ciência, Rua da Quinta Grande 6, 2780-156 Oeiras, Portugal

Iron is critical in host-microbe interactions, and its availability is tightly regulated in the mammalian gut. Antibiotics and inflammation can perturb iron availability in the gut, which could alter host-microbe interactions. Here, we show that an adaptive allele of iscR, a major regulator of iron homeostasis of Escherichia coli, is under fluctuating selection in the mouse gut. In vivo competitions in immune-competent, immune-compromised, and germ-free mice reveal that the selective pressure on an iscR mutant E. coli is modulated by the presence of antibiotics, the microbiota, and the immune system. In vitro assays show that iron availability is an important mediator of the iscR allele fitness benefits or costs. We identify Lipocalin-2, a host’s immune protein that prevents bacterial iron acquisition, as a major host mechanism underlying fluctuating selection of iscR. Our results provide a remarkable example of strong fluctuating selection acting on bacterial iron regulation in the mammalian gut.

 

Marco António Dias Louro

Keywords: Cell Biology, Evolution

Unravelling the population dynamics of centrosome amplification

Marco António Dias Louro, Mónica Bettencourt-Dias, Claudia Bank

Instituto Gulbenkian de Ciência

Understanding the causes and consequences of organelle copy number variation is a fundamental problem in biology. For certain subcellular structures, such as centrioles, copy number is near invariant in healthy proliferating cells, ranging from two to four depending on the cell cycle stage. In cancer cells, centriole number tends to be higher, a phenomenon termed centrosome amplification, but widely heterogeneous. How this intra- and intercellular variation is determined, and how it affects cellular evolution, remain poorly understood.
